How to Read a Book

Posted by Lap on Friday, September 30, 2022

Introduction

Two kind of books:

  • Fiction/novels books
  • Non-fiction books
    • textbooks
    • manuals
    • journal articles
    • histories
    • academic studies
    • etc
The purpose of reading things like Non-fiction books is to gain, and retain, information

finding out what happens — as quickly and easily as possible — is your main goal

NEVER read a non-fiction book or article from beginning to end

  • Strategy
    • should ALWAYS jump ahead
    • skip around
    • use every available strategy to
      • discover,
      • then to understand
      • and finally to remember what the writer has to say

you should be able to read a 300-page book in 06 to 08 hours

Reading strategies and techniques

1. Read the whole thing

  • Major arguments and evidence matter more than details.
  • Grasping the structure of the whole is more important than reading every word.

2. Decide how much time you will spend

  • If you know exactly how long you can actually spend on reading, you can plan how much time to devote to each item
  • Never start to read without planning when to stop

3. Have a purpose and a strategy

  • why you are reading this book
  • how you are going to read it?
    1. find out four things
      • Who is the author?
      • What are the book’s arguments?
      • What is the evidence that supports these?
      • What are the book’s conclusions?
    2. trying to determine
      • What are the weaknesses of these arguments, evidence, and conclusions?
      • What do you think about the arguments, evidence, and conclusions?
      • How does (or how could) the author respond to these weaknesses, and to your own criticisms?
    3. By the time you finish, you should be able to answer them all
      • Imagine that you’re going to review the book for a magazine.
      • Imagine that you’re having a conversation, or a formal debate, with the author.
      • Imagine an examination on the book. What would the questions be, and how would you answer them?

4. Read actively

  • Never rely on the author’s structures alone.
  • Move around in the text, following your own goals.
  • Constantly generate hypotheses
    • the main point of the book is that…
    • How does the author know that…?
  • Try to confirm your hypotheses and answer your questions. Once you finish, review these.

5. Read it three times

1. Overview: discovery (5-10 percent of total time)

  • read very quickly. The *goal is to discover the book
  • Find out the writer’s purpose, methods, and conclusions
    • Mark: headings, passages, and phrases that seem important
      • without reading carefully
    • Generate questions to answer on your second reading
      • what does term or phrase X mean?
      • Why doesn’t the author cover subject Y?
      • Who is Z?

2. Detail: understanding (70-80 percent of total time)

  • The goal is understanding
    • get a careful, critical, thoughtful grasp of the key points
    • evaluate the author’s evidence for his/her points.
  • Focus on
    • beginnings
    • ends of chapters and
    • major sections.
  • Pay special attention to the passages you marked on the first round.
    • Try to answer any questions you generated on the first round.

3. Notes: recall and note-taking (10-20 percent of total time)

  • make brief notes about the arguments, evidence, and conclusions
  • The goal here is to process the material by translating into your own mental framework, which means using your own words as much as possible.

1-3 pages of notes per 100 pages of text is a good goal to shoot for

For a 250-page book, I might spend

  • 15 minutes on overview,
  • 4 hours on detailed reading, and
  • 20-30 minutes making notes

6. Focus on parts with high information content

  • Tables of contents
  • Pictures
  • Charts
  • Headings, and
  • Conclusion
  • Chapter introductions and conclusions

7. Use PTML (personal text markup language)

  • Mark up your reading with your own notes. This helps you
    • learn and also
    • helps you find important passages later.

8. Know the author(s) and organizations

  • Authors are people with backgrounds and biases. They work in organizations that give them context and depth.
    • What shaped the author’s intellectual perspective?
    • What is his or her profession?
    • Is the author an academic, a journalist, a professional (doctor, lawyer, industrial scientist, etc.)?
    • Expertise?
    • Other books and articles?
    • Intellectual network(s)?
    • Race?
    • Class?
    • Political affiliation?
    • Why did the author decide to write this book?
      • When?
      • For what audience(s)?
      • Who paid for the research work (private foundations, government grant agencies, industrial sponsors, etc.)?
      • Who wrote “jacket blurbs” in support of the book?

9. Know the intellectual context

  • A book is almost always partly a response to other writers
  • Figure out what, and whom, it is answering.
  • Pay special attention to points where the author tells you directly that s/he is disagreeing with others:
    • Conventional wisdom holds that x, but I argue instead that y. Questions:
      • Is x really conventional wisdom?
      • Among what group of people?
    • Famous Jane Scholar says that x, but I will show that y Questions:
      • Who’s Famous Jane, and why do other people believe her?
      • How plausible are x and y?
      • Is the author straining to find something original to say, or has s/he genuinely convinced you that Famous Jane is wrong?

10. Use your unconscious mind

  • Leave time between reading sessions for your mind to process the material.

11. Rehearse, and use multiple modes

  • Talking,
  • Visualizing, or
  • Writing about what you’ve read helps you remember it

Practices
